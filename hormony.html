hormony Case Study

The Challenge:

How can we create an inclusive tool that allows people who menstruate to feel more in control of and connected to their personal hormonal health?

"Female patients and people of color are more likely to have their symptoms dismissed by medical providers," reads a recent New York Times article. Female reproductive health is under recognized, prompting women to self-advocate and search for their own unique solutions.

Analyzing the current market:

According to white papers and articles regarding the current FemTech market:

  • 75% of people with periods do not understand their hormones
  • 80% of women suffer from hormonal imbalances and
  • More than half of women believe depression is a "normal part of aging."

Luckily, we are currently experiencing a FemTech revolution. According to McKinsey, forecasts suggest opportunities for double-digit revenue growth with many white spaces in the cross section of menstrual tracking and female health.

A competitor SWOT analysis allowed me to identify potential opportunities that seem to be under-utilized by competitors such as:

  • Entering the LGBTQ+ space
  • Clarify that information and articles are fact-driven and vetted
  • Adding a community feature such as a chat features

Key Takeaways:

  • FemTech's current market size ranges from $500 million to $1 billion.
  • There are significant white spaces in FemTech particularly in menstrual health and tracking because competitors specialize in specific conditions.
  • Competitors, such as Clue, lead with menstrual tracking focused on ovulation, fertility and menstrual flow.
  • There are need gaps with current competitor products.

Understanding the User

Using quantitative research methods to collect general data, I conducted 2 separate anonymous surveys with a total of 45 unique responders. Following this, I continued by conducting qualitative research in order to understand the motivations and needs of my user. I conducted 5 user interviews of women between the age of 34-44.

Key Takeaways:

  • 50% of survey respondents suffered from PMS and 8% suffer from PMDD
  • Women between the ages of 26-42 don't want to input data into their phones daily.
  • There was a spectrum of potential users - from those who have regular periods with a limited amount of physical or emotional side effects to those with irregular periods with mild to severe physical and emotional side effects - who would be interested in a hormone app.
  • There was unanimous interest in understanding how periods relate to actual hormones.
  • There's distrust in doctors to inform about hormone related questions, because of lack of specialty and/or empathy.
  • All enjoyed the visual elements of comparing cycles and liked the assistance of visual aides to make intuition more concrete to validate knowledge.

As the potential users displayed a varying spectrum of personality traits, I defined the following important user characterizations:

  1. Menstruation - either regularly or irregularly
  2. Experience imbalance and hormonal flux
  3. Want information in one centralized location
  4. Experience discomfort and want to know "why"

How to improve the existing hormone tracking web space

Synthesizing findings from user interviews, surveys and the competitor analyses, it became clear that the following key takeaways would define the functionality of the web application.

Tracking, while important, cannot be the focus of this app because that already exists and should be a secondary functionality. Users will most likely only track when there is an ailment or problem that breaks homeostasis. = Flexible Tracking

There is so much misinformation and general lack of knowledge about hormonal health. Many people want to know more, especially as they notice more and more hormonal shifts. = Vetted Learning

Many people ask their friends, family and online forums before asking their doctors for support because of distrust or lack of genuine attention. But online forums and friends aren't specialists and can lack focus. = Monitored community chats

Mapping User's Behaviour

To synthesis real-life scenarios where users would need access to hormony, I developed user journeys in order to better understand how and when this app could fit into users' lives.

In order to validate navigation principles, user scenarios and task flows were explored to visualize how users would interact with the web app. These flows enabled me to focus on the Minimal Viable Product scenario and happy path in order to develop important functionality and troubleshoot ideas.

Key takeaways:

  • User's will search for assistance when they are distressed
  • Most use scenarios will be multi-faceted with use of 2+ main features

How Might We...

Now that I understand users' motivations and needs and have a base understanding of the market, I was able to solidify my problem statement in order to begin developing hormony:

Our users need a way to approach, understand and directly address the symptoms of female-hormonal shifts because by understanding the causes and effects of hormonal health-physically, mentally and emotionally-the user can help themselves to reduce hormonal disruption to lead a more balanced life

This will be achieved with vetted educational and newspaper articles, a robust community forum, and flexible personal tracking and note-taking abilities.

Building hormony

Information Architecture: After many iterations defining the website's main functionality and two rounds of closed card sorting, the site map started to come together in a way that was both intuitive and in-depth. It was important that there would be available content in the not-logged-in state. Data privacy was also considered in order to build the site in a way that would reduce discomfort, hacking and leaks.

Mobile-First: Research let me to a mobile-first approach for this responsive web app. Why? Because Millennials and Gen X audiences are tech-savvy and are attached to their phones. Additionally, the research I conducted concluded that most people prefer to input personal information regarding health and menstruation using the privacy of their personal mobile devices.

Designing form to follow function:

From Lo-fidelity to mid-fidelity wireframes: Beginning with low-fidelity wireframe sketches, I started to conceptualize the functionality of the website starting in a mobile scenario. Working mobile-first allowed me to set size minimums early and see how the website would look in the highest case-usage possibility.

Testing, testing and more testing

A first round of usability testing helped test if navigational paths made sense and was intuitive. This process allowed me to check NNG's Usability Heuristics and easily identify pain points that needed immediate attention. Using Affinity mapping and Rainbow mapping, I was able to identify critical errors using the Jakob Nielsen's severity rating to correct in the high-fidelity prototype.

Learnings from User Testing:

After revisions, which focused on accessibility, green design practices and Material Design / iOS design recommendations, I asked colleagues to peer review the web app before preparing the MVP for beta launch.

Biggest challenges:

  • The Health Log information architecture evolved from living within the same webpage, to two separate features thereby allowing easier and more intuitive navigation.
  • Navigation is visible from both tasks
  • With additional CTAs, users have more control to navigate freely

Adjusted Key Features:

  • Global Navigation (making sure necessary systems are visible)
  • Ease of Editability
  • Simplification of colors

High-Fidelity Prototype:

From usability test results, and continual reiterations, I implemented suggested and researched changes across the entire prototype in order to prepare hormony for beta-launch.

Visual Design Development:

With the use of multiple methods of design theory and development-Gestalt visual design principals, Material Design 3 & User feedback-I developed the visual design for hormony.

I created a Brand Guideline to define all the visual elements together. In order to create the brand, it was important to first define the voice, tone and visuals. Non-gendered colors and imagery were chosen in order to create a neutral and approachable aesthetic. The tone is empowering, smart, succinct and educated.

Next Steps

This concept fills a real need in the hormonal health tracking market, and I intend to speak with developers and specialists to talk about the viability of building out hormony.

I'm also considering additional features. In the original iteration of hormony, one of the key functionalities was an expert advice feature to allow users access to a hormonal health specialist. As this would require more research, vetting, and policy knowledge, and users were unenthusiastic about virtual appointments with specialists, this feature was removed. As mobile healthcare continues due to the ongoing pandemic and the need for specialists-who may be in different parts of the country (or world)-continues to grow, expert advice/assistance could be reconsidered during an upcoming phase or iteration.

Learnings and Reflections

While executing hormony, from idea to prototype, it became clear that the UX concepts I used to create this design were similar to the conceptual approaches I've used in my 12 years as a graphic design professional. Researching primary and secondary sources, holding and attending workshops, sketching, convergent ideation and constant iteration are techniques I've always utilized when defining design problems and executing solutions.

Due to my constant curiosity and continual questioning process, I designed different iterations of this app with varying focuses because of the gaping holes in hormonal healthcare and need from the female community. My research into hormonal health was at times infuriating and humbling as I discovered how hard it is for people with ovaries to find appropriate medical and professional assistance. Women's healthcare is overlooked too often-the statistics and public stories are there to corroborate that fact.

I feel fortunate that I had the time to explore and implement these iterations. If I were to do it again, I would implement the following learnings:

  • Define goals as soon as the research is concrete enough to back them up
  • Better done, than perfect
  • Stay curious, open-minded and go with the flow...
